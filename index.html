Rent a car at the airport Not optional. Estreito da Calheta is 45 km west of Funchal (about 50 minutes on the VR1), and everything worth seeing is up a mountain or on another coast. Pick the smallest car that fits the group. Desks inside the FNC terminal.
Airport to Estreito da Calheta About 1h15 by car on the VR1. Straight through Funchal, Câmara de Lobos, Ribeira Brava, then up to Calheta. Signposted all the way. No tolls. Taxi around 90 to 110 euros if nobody drives.
The VR1 is your spine Free motorway along the south coast. From Calheta it runs east through Funchal to the airport at Machico, and west to Ponta do Sol and Ribeira Brava. Most journeys will start and end on it.
Tunnels and gradients Over 150 tunnels on the island, several over 3 km (Encumeada, Faial). Headlights on, always. Gradients above 25 percent are normal. Use lower gears going down, not the brakes.
Bolt in Funchal only Bolt and Uber run in greater Funchal. Forget it for Estreito da Calheta, coverage is thin to nonexistent out here. If nobody wants to drive after a dinner out, plan for taxis or a designated driver.
Currency and cards Euro everywhere. Cards accepted in almost every restaurant and cafe, even small ones. Apple Pay and Google Pay work across the island.
Bring cash anyway Around 100 euros in small notes for the week. The Rabaçal shuttle bus is cash only (5 euros one way), plus tips, the odd market stall, and the cable car if card machines are down.
Tipping is light Round up at cafes, 5 to 10 percent at dinner for good service. Nothing is expected, tips are a thank you, not a wage supplement.
Couvert is not free The bread, olives, butter, cheese, and pate that land unasked on the table are charged per item eaten. Wave away what you do not want. It is not rude, it is expected.
South coast (Funchal) 20 to 25°C daytime, 18 to 20°C at night. Sunny most days. Sea around 21°C, cold at first and fine after a minute.
North coast and peaks North coast similar temperatures but cloudier, with brief showers likely on at least one day. Peaks (Arieiro, Ruivo) can drop to 8 to 12°C with strong wind even when Funchal is 24°C.
Microclimate is real Funchal can be in full sun while São Vicente is in fog and Arieiro is above the clouds. Check the Pico do Arieiro live webcam before driving up. If it is grey, do something else that day.
Saharan haze happens Once or twice a summer, dust from the Sahara rolls in and flattens the sky to a beige haze for a day or two. Views suffer. Nothing to do about it except adjust plans to coast and food.
Pack list Light layers, a proper waterproof shell, trail shoes or runners with grip, swimwear, strong sun protection. UV here is stronger than mainland Portugal. A warm mid-layer for the peaks, even in June.
Plug type F, 230V Same two round pins as continental Europe. Bring a UK to EU adapter from home, easier than hunting one down in Funchal. Voltage is the same as the UK so no transformer needed.
eSIM, not roaming Airalo or Saily, 10 to 15 euros for 5 GB for the week. Activate before you land. Coverage is solid along the south coast, patchy in the north mountains and in laurisilva valleys.
Download offline maps Google Maps offline region covering the whole island is about 200 MB. Do this on hotel Wi-Fi before driving into the mountains or the north coast.
SIMplifica trail booking Since January 2026, all 42 official trails require advance booking at simplifica.madeira.gov.pt. Pick the PR number, pick a 30-minute entry slot, pay. Standard trails 4.50 euros per person, PR1 Arieiro to Ruivo 10.50 euros. Rangers check QR codes on the trail and fines are around 50 euros on the spot. Book 7 to 14 days ahead for sunrise slots and weekends.
Car rental Late June is peak. Book 6 to 8 weeks out. Local firms like Guerin and Rodavante are often cheaper than the big internationals, with desks at the airport.
Dinner reservations O Polar and O Lagar in Câmara de Lobos take bookings by phone only. Call a day ahead in summer or you will wait an hour. Armazém do Sal in Funchal takes bookings online.
Monte cable car and toboggan Same-day tickets at the lower station are fine, no booking required. Cable car up around 18 euros return, toboggan down around 35 euros per two-person sled.
Speed radars are everywhere 50 km/h in towns, 80 km/h on main roads, 100 km/h on the VR1. Fixed radars on the motorway are well signposted, but fines reach the UK via the hire car company. Do not risk it.
Trailhead parking fills early Rabaçal, Pico do Arieiro, and Fanal are full by 09:00 in summer. Arrive before sunrise for the light and the space, or after 15:00 when the tour buses have cleared.
Funchal parking Central Funchal is painful for cars. Use paid garages (Anadia Shopping, Almirante Reis, Campo da Barca) and walk. Around 2 euros per hour, capped at 12 to 15 euros overnight.
Fuel is cheaper here Madeira fuel prices are lower than mainland Portugal thanks to regional tax breaks. Fill the tank once near the start of the week, that is usually enough for the island.

Food Moments
Espetada (the real one)
Beef skewered on a bay laurel stick, grilled over wood, hung from a hook at the table, carved as you eat. The Câmara de Lobos version is the benchmark.
Bolo do caco with garlic butter
Flat sweet-potato bread, split open while still warm, slathered with garlic-parsley butter. Try it on its own first, then as a prego sandwich.
Poncha at a backstreet poncheira
Aguardente de cana, honey, lemon juice, crushed by a wooden pestle (the mexelote). Drunk neat. Try the traditional poncha first, then the passion fruit version.
Peixe-espada preto com banana
Black scabbard fish, caught off Madeira only, with fried banana on top. Sounds strange, works. A Madeira signature you will not eat on the mainland.
A proper bica and pastel de nata
Espresso plus pastel de nata at a local café is the morning ritual, 2 to 3 euros. Never skip. Bonus: the Madeiran nata has cinnamon on top.
A glass of real Madeira wine
Fortified wine aged by heat (estufagem). Sercial and Verdelho are drier, Bual and Malvasia are sweet. Try a flight at Blandy's in Funchal.

Good to Know

The things guidebooks skip. Opinionated where it helps.

Espetada em pau de louro Beef cubes rubbed with garlic, salt, and bay leaf, skewered on a bay laurel branch, grilled over charcoal, and served hanging from a hook. The real version uses a wooden skewer, not a metal one. Order it at O Polar, O Lagar, Casa Madeirense, or Chalet Vicente.
Bolo do caco A flat round wheat bread baked on a hot basalt stone, served warm and split open with garlic-parsley butter melted inside. Comes alongside espetada, or on its own as a snack, or built into a prego sandwich with a steak. It is not actually a cake.
Milho frito and picado Milho frito is chunks of fried cornmeal, the island's answer to chips, and the correct side for espetada. Picado is small cubes of beef stir-fried with garlic, peppers, and chips piled on top, meant for sharing and drinking beer with.
Espada (black scabbard fish) Cultural mention rather than recommendation. The long-bodied deep-water fish caught off Câmara de Lobos, often served with a slice of fried banana. Looks alarming when raw (all teeth and eye) and is flaky-mild when cooked. You will see it on most menus.
Poncha, the real one Sugarcane rum (aguardente), honey, and lemon juice, muddled together with a wooden tool called a mexelote, served in a small glass. That is it. Tourist bars add orange, passionfruit, tangerine, none of which are traditional. Ask for the original and drink it in the afternoon, not after dinner.
Nikita and Coral Nikita is a strange and wonderful local drink: white wine, pineapple, vanilla ice cream, and a splash of beer, blended. Invented in Câmara de Lobos in the 1980s. Coral is the local lager, brewed in Funchal since 1872 and the default house beer. Both are cheap.
Bolo de mel and queijadas Bolo de mel is a dense dark fruit cake spiced with cinnamon and made with sugarcane molasses (not honey, despite the name), kept for months and cut with fingers, never a knife. Queijadas are small cheesecakes from Funchal, best from Fábrica Santo António on Travessa do Forno. Both are Christmas baking that happens year-round.
Madeira wine Fortified wine made on the island since the 1500s, historically shipped as ballast on trade routes, which is why heat and age made it rather than ruined it. The four classic grape names go from dry to sweet: Sercial, Verdelho, Bual, Malvasia. Try a flight at Blandy's Wine Lodge in Funchal or at Pereira d'Oliveira.
Meals are late Locals eat lunch between 13:00 and 14:30, dinner between 20:00 and 22:00. Arriving at a restaurant at 19:00 for dinner means you will be served by the cleaning crew. Most kitchens open for dinner at 19:30.
Wave away the couvert When bread, olives, butter, cheese, and pâté land on the table unasked, they are not free. You pay per item eaten. Touch only what you want, send the rest back with a smile. It is not rude, it is the expected protocol.
Drive on the right, yield early On the single-lane mountain roads, the driver going uphill has right of way. Back up to the nearest pull-out if you meet someone coming up. Two flashes of headlights from the other driver means thank you, flash back.
Bom dia goes a long way Enter any shop, cafe, bakery, or bar with a loud bom dia (morning), boa tarde (afternoon), or boa noite (evening). Older locals expect it, and even a cheerful mispronounced version earns goodwill. Leaving without an obrigado (thank you) is genuinely rude.
The sea is colder than you think Madeira is not the Algarve. The south coast sits around 21°C in late June, the north coast two to three degrees less. It is refreshing after a hike, and you do acclimatise, but the first 30 seconds are a shock.
There are no real beaches Madeira is a volcanic island with cliffs going straight into the sea. Almost every beach is either imported sand (Machico, Calheta) or volcanic pebbles. If you are expecting soft yellow sand, adjust expectations. Go to Porto Santo for that, a 2 hour ferry away.
Distances feel longer On a map, Funchal to Porto Moniz looks like a 30 minute drive. It is closer to 75 minutes by the modern tunnel road, longer if you take the scenic coast. The roads climb, curve, and tunnel. Plan drive times at around 50 km/h average, not 90.
The weather line is real Funchal can be in 25°C sun while the pass at Encumeada is in 12°C rain. 20 minutes of driving, different planet. If a mountain day is booked and you wake up to fog on the hills, sometimes it clears by 10:00, sometimes it stays. Check the IFCN webcams before committing.
The airport is legendary for a reason Madeira's runway is built on stilts over the sea and sits between two cliffs with strong crosswinds. Pilots need a special rating to land here. Diversions to Porto Santo or back to Lisbon do happen. Keep the itinerary loose on arrival and departure days.
🌋 This is Portugal, but it is its own country Madeira has its own regional government, its own laws on fishing and trails, its own cuisine, its own wine, and an accent that mainland Portuguese find thick. Do not compare it to Lisbon or the Algarve. Compare it to the Azores, or the Canaries, or nothing at all.
🏔️ The island has two faces. See both. The south is dry, warm, and where the hotels and postcards live. The north is lush, wet, waterfall-lined, and feels like a different country. If you only see Funchal and the south, you will leave thinking Madeira is a nice beach alternative. Drive north at least once, ideally for a full day.
🎨 Monte Palace beats the Jardim Botânico Funchal has two major gardens, the old Jardim Botânico and the younger Monte Palace. Monte Palace is more interesting, has better views, and has the azulejo history collection. If you only visit one, make it Monte.
🍷 Go back to Câmara de Lobos for the second drink Most tour buses stop at Câmara de Lobos in the early afternoon for 40 minutes, take the Churchill photo, and leave. The village comes alive at 18:00 when locals arrive for poncha at the backstreet poncheiras near the harbour. That is when to be there.
Cabo Girão viewpoint Europe's tallest sea cliff with a glass-floored skywalk, totally free. Most people think it costs money because it looks like a built attraction. It does not. Drive up, walk out over 580 m of air, drive down.
Pontinha breakwater at sunset The long concrete pier in Funchal harbour. Walk to the end 30 minutes before sunset and look back at the amphitheatre of the city lighting up. Best free view in Funchal and almost no one does it.
Doca do Cavacas rock pools Natural rock pools on the Funchal seafront just west of Praia Formosa, cut into the volcanic rock. Free to use, calm enough for a swim, cold enough to wake you up. Under the radar because everyone books the paid complex next door.
Rua de Santa Maria doors The painted doors of the old town are free to look at. Walk the full length of the street once in daylight for the art, once at night for the street scene. Some doors have changed three times since 2011, so guidebook photos are often out of date.
